# Summary

Traditional competitive defences rested on scarce resources: expert research, elite engineers, seasoned marketers, well-trained sales teams. Scarcity created cost, and cost created the barrier that discouraged would-be challengers. [Generative AI](../../site/concepts/generative-ai.md) attacks that scarcity premium on every front at once: research compression (days of competitive analysis in hours), code acceleration (agentic assistants drafting complex modules for the many, not the few), content on tap (image, video and copy for pennies), and AI-augmented sales and support automating the human-heavy funnel.

# The consequence

The *time-and-effort tax* that used to fund a moat is collapsing. Basic product development and basic go-to-market are no longer expensive, exclusive endeavours — so defences built on velocity and headcount stop defending. Scarcity hasn't disappeared, though; it has relocated (see [Moats After AI](./moats-after-ai.md)), and the classic search for uncontested water gets redrawn with it (see [Blue Ocean Strategy](./blue-ocean-strategy.md)).

From the article [AI Impact — Moats After the Scarcity Collapse](https://davidjcrawford.github.io/Portfolio/writing/ai-impact-moats-after-the-scarcity/).

# Core idea

The cost barrier that funded moats is collapsing; strategy has to find where scarcity moved.
