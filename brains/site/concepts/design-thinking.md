# Summary

A human-centred approach to innovation, associated with IDEO (David Kelley, Tim Brown) and the Stanford d.school. Tim Brown's own definition gives it away: innovation that integrates *the needs of people, the possibilities of technology, and the requirements for business success* — which is precisely the [3 Lenses of Innovation](./the-3-lenses-of-innovation.md), [desirability](./desirability.md), [feasibility](./feasibility.md) and [viability](./viability.md). Those three lenses are Design Thinking's core frame.

# How it works

It is a method and a toolkit more than a philosophy: empathise with the people you are designing for, define the problem, ideate widely, prototype, and test — iterating toward something that lands in the overlap of the three lenses. It is deliberately human-centred and convergent, moving from understanding people toward a designed artefact.

# Not the same as transdisciplinary design

The two overlap heavily and are easy to conflate, but they are distinct. Design Thinking is a human-centred *method* that converges on a solution through the three lenses. [Transdisciplinary design](./transdisciplinary-design.md) is a wider *perspective*: it starts from a (often wicked) problem rather than a picture of the finished thing, transcends any single discipline, and lets the solution's form emerge from the problem space. You can run Design Thinking's toolkit inside a transdisciplinary stance — they operate at different levels.

# Core idea

A human-centred method for innovation, organised around desirability, feasibility and viability.
