# Summary

Traditional UX workflow is *painting*: plan, lay down a composition, refine, hand off. Tools like Figma are superb for clarity and systems, but they pull you toward designing representations of software instead of designing in software. Agentic coding tools pull the other way — they make it realistic to build something you can click, break and *feel*, early. That's *sculpting*: get something, poke at it, remove parts, rearrange, do it again a few times, then pick the right one.

# Why it matters

Prototyping has become dramatically cheaper, and that changes the economics of design decisions: it's one thing to argue about a flow in a static mock, another to put two working versions in front of someone and watch where they hesitate. And when generating options is cheap, taste becomes the bottleneck — which is exactly where [curation](./curation.md) takes over, and the build-to-think stance [David Kelley](../people/david-kelley.md) called creative confidence stops being optional. More options, tested for real, is how [desirability](./desirability.md) actually gets settled.

# The agentic design studio

One way to run it, with [agentic AI](./agentic-ai.md): a small studio of helpers — a brief helper that tightens the problem statement and constraints, a variant generator that produces options inside your system primitives, and a critic that stress-tests flows and edge cases. The designer's job moves upstream: not producing the most, but choosing the right direction, for the right reasons.

Explored at length in [From Painting to Sculpting](https://davidjcrawford.github.io/Portfolio/writing/from-painting-to-sculpting/).

# Core idea

Design in software, not in pictures of software — generate options cheaply, then curate hard.
