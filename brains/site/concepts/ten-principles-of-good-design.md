# Summary

[Dieter Rams](../people/dieter-rams.md) framed these in the late 1970s, asking himself *is my design good design?* They read as ten tests, and they translate cleanly from Braun hardware to software — which is why they are among the most-cited guidelines in digital product design.

# The ten

Good design is:

1. **Innovative** — it never exhausts the possibilities, and it advances alongside technology.
2. **Useful** — it serves the product's purpose; everything else is subordinate to that.
3. **Aesthetic** — well-made things can be beautiful, and beauty affects how we feel and function.
4. **Understandable** — it clarifies the product, ideally makes it self-explanatory.
5. **Unobtrusive** — it is neutral and restrained, leaving room for the user.
6. **Honest** — it doesn't over-promise or manipulate.
7. **Long-lasting** — it avoids being fashionable, so it never feels dated.
8. **Thorough down to the last detail** — nothing is arbitrary or left to chance.
9. **Environmentally friendly** — it conserves resources and minimises harm.
10. **As little design as possible** — less, but better; concentrate on the essential.

# Why here

Principles 8 and 10 are the two I lean on hardest — they are the heart of [Less, but Better](./less-but-better.md) — and the full list is a working definition of [product](./product.md) as a craft.

# Core idea

Ten tests for good design, ending where restraint lives: as little design as possible.
