# Summary

A way of working that transcends disciplines rather than sitting inside any one of them. It integrates knowledge from many fields — and from outside academia, with the stakeholders and communities actually affected — to take on complex, *wicked* problems that no single discipline can frame on its own. It is an orientation more than a fixed method.

# A matter of perspective

The distinction I hold onto: most design disciplines start with a picture of the finished thing and work toward making something in that shape. Transdisciplinary design starts from the *problem* and lets the form of the solution emerge from the problem space. The designer works more as a facilitator than a solver, staying open to interpretation rather than committed to a predetermined answer — which is exactly what suits it to wicked problems that have no clean, final solution.

# Not the same as Design Thinking

It overlaps with [Design Thinking](./design-thinking.md) but is not the same thing. Design Thinking is a human-centred *method* that converges on a solution through the [3 lenses](./the-3-lenses-of-innovation.md); transdisciplinary design is the wider *perspective* that method can sit inside. It pairs naturally with [systems thinking](./systems-thinking.md), and it is how you reason about [Nth-order effects](./nth-order-effects.md): no single discipline sees the whole system.

# In practice

I do [advisory work with the University of Waikato](../situations/school-of-transdisciplinary-design.md) — across its School of Design and School of Computing — on transdisciplinary design and on how to teach agentic development as a discipline.

# Core idea

Start from the problem, not a picture of the finished thing, and let the solution's form emerge.
